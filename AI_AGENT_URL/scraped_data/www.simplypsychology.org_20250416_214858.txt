<div class="col-sm-1 hidden-xs hidden-print"><!-- Grey space --></div>
<div class="col-sm-10 hidden-print"><span style="background-color: var(--base-3); font-size: inherit; text-transform: initial;">The brain receives information from sensory receptors and sends messages to muscles and glands. It is the center of all conscious awareness and is divided into different lobes with different functions. It contains the cerebrum, about 85% of the total mass.</span></div>
</div>
<div class="row">
<div class="col-md-7 col-lg-6 col-sm-10">
<blockquote>
<p>The brain controls all functions of the body, interprets information from the outside world, and defines who we are as individuals and how we experience the world.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>The brain receives information through our senses: sight, touch, taste, smell, and hearing. This information is processed in the brain, allowing us to give meaning to the input it receives.</p>
<p>The brain is part of the central nervous system (<a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/central-nervous-system.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">CNS</a>) along with the spinal cord. There is also a peripheral nervous system (PNS) comprised of <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/spinal-nerves-anatomy.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">31 pairs of spinal nerves</a> that branch from the spinal cord and cranial nerves that branch from the brain.</p>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Brain-Parts"></span>Brain Parts<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<blockquote>
<p>The brain is composed of the cerebrum, cerebellum, and brainstem (Fig. 1).</p>
</blockquote>
<p><img fetchpriority="high" decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail  aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/brain-structure.png" alt="The brain is composed of the cerebrum, cerebellum, and brainstem" width="670" height="449" data-pin-id="889742470119352003" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 1"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 1.</em> The brain has three main parts: the cerebrum, cerebellum, and brainstem.</small></p>
<h3>Cerebrum</h3>
<p>The cerebrum is the largest and most recognizable part of the brain. It consists of <a href="what-is-grey-matter-in-the-brain.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">grey matter</a> (the <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/what-is-the-cerebral-cortex.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">cerebral cortex</a> ) and <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/what-is-white-matter-in-the-brain.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">white matter</a> at the center. The cerebrum is divided into two hemispheres, the left and right, and contains the lobes of the brain (frontal, temporal, parietal, and occipital lobes).</p>
<p>The cerebrum produces higher functioning roles such as thinking, learning, memory, language, emotion, movement, and perception.</p>
<h3>The Cerebellum</h3>
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/what-is-the-cerebellum.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">cerebellum</a> is located under the cerebrum and monitors and regulates motor behaviors, especially automatic movements.</p>
<p>This structure is also important for regulating posture and balance and has recently been suggested for being involved in learning and attention.</p>
<p>Although the cerebellum only accounts for roughly 10% of the brain’s total weight, this area is thought to contain more neurons (nerve cells) than the rest of the brain combined.</p>
<h3>Brainstem</h3>
<p>The brainstem is located at the base of the brain. This area connects the cerebrum and the cerebellum to the spinal cord, acting as a relay station for these areas.</p>
<p>The brainstem regulates automatic functions such as sleep cycles, breathing, body temperature, digestion, coughing, and sneezing.</p>
</div>
</div>
</div>


</p>
<figure class="gb-block-image gb-block-image-7afebdba"><img decoding="async" width="1902" height="1902" class="gb-image gb-image-7afebdba" title="Brain Stem" src="https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem.jpeg" alt="A diagram of the brain stem with the anatomical parts labelled: Thalamus, midbrain, pons, medulla and spinal cord" srcset="https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem.jpeg 1902w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem-300x300.jpeg 300w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem-1024x1024.jpeg 1024w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem-150x150.jpeg 150w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem-768x768.jpeg 768w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/brain-stem-1536x1536.jpeg 1536w" sizes="(max-width: 1902px) 100vw, 1902px" /></figure>
<p>


<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Right-Brain-vs-Left-Brain"></span>Right Brain vs. Left Brain<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>The cerebrum is divided into two halves: the <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/left-brain-vs-right-brain.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">right and left hemispheres</a> (Fig. 2). The left hemisphere controls the right half of the body, and the right hemisphere controls the left half.</p>
<p>The two hemispheres are connected by a thick band of neural fibers known as the corpus callosum, consisting of about 200 million axons.</p>
<p>The corpus callosum allows the two hemispheres to communicate and allows information being processed on one side of the brain to be shared with the other.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail  aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/CerebralHemispheres.png" alt="The cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres. The two sides are connected by the nerve fibers corpus callosum." width="1902" height="1902" data-pin-id="889742470119243765" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 2"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 2.</em> The cerebrum is divided into left and right hemispheres. The nerve fibers corpus callosum connects the two sides.</small></p>
<p><a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/brain-lateralization.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">Hemispheric lateralization</a> is the idea that each hemisphere is responsible for different functions. Each of these functions is localized to either the right or left side.</p>
<p>The left hemisphere is associated with language functions, such as formulating grammar and vocabulary and containing different language centers (Broca’s and Wernicke’s area).</p>
<p>The right hemisphere is associated with more visuospatial functions such as visualization, depth perception, and spatial navigation. These left and right functions are the case in most people, especially those who are right-handed.</p>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Lobes-of-the-Brain"></span>Lobes of the Brain<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>Each cerebral hemisphere can be subdivided into four lobes, each associated with different functions.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The four lobes of the brain are the frontal, parietal, temporal, and occipital lobes (Figure 3).</p>
</blockquote>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/cerebral-hemispheres.jpg" alt="cerebral hemispheres: Frontal lobes, Occipital lobes, Parietal lobes, Temporal lobes" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 3"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 3.</em> The cerebrum is divided into four lobes: frontal, parietal, occipital, and temporal.</small></p>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Frontal lobes</h3>
<p><span style="font-size: inherit; background-color: var(--base-3); color: var(--contrast-2);">The </span><a style="font-size: inherit; background-color: var(--base-3);" href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/frontal-lobe.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">frontal lobes</a><span style="font-size: inherit; background-color: var(--base-3); color: var(--contrast-2);"> are located at the front of the brain, behind the forehead (Figure 4). </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: inherit; background-color: var(--base-3); color: var(--contrast-2);">Their main functions are associated with higher cognitive functions, including problem-solving, decision-making, attention, intelligence, and voluntary behaviors.</span></p>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The frontal lobes contain the <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/motor-cortex.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">motor cortex</a> responsible for planning and coordinating movements.</p>
<p>It also contains the prefrontal cortex, which is responsible for initiating higher-lever cognitive functioning, and Broca’s Area, which is essential for language production.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/frontal-lobe.jpg" alt="frontal lobe structure" width="959" height="665" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 4"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 4.</em> Frontal lobe structure.</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Temporal lobes</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/temporal-lobe.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">temporal lobes</a> are located on both sides of the brain, near the temples of the head, hence the name temporal lobes (Figure 5).</p>
<p>The main functions of these lobes include understanding, language, memory acquisition, face recognition, object recognition, perception, and auditory information processing.</p>
<p>There is a temporal lobe in both the left and right hemispheres. The left temporal lobe, which is usually the most dominant in people, is associated with language, learning, memorizing, forming words, and remembering verbal information.</p>
<p>The left lobe also contains a vital language center known as Wernicke’s area, which is essential for language development. The right temporal lobe is usually associated with learning and memorizing non-verbal information and determining facial expressions.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/temporal-lobe-structure.jpg" alt="temporal lobe structure" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 5"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 5.</em> Temporal lobe structure.</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Parietal lobes</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/parietal-lobe.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">parietal lobe</a> is located at the top of the brain, between the frontal and occipital lobes, and above the temporal lobes (Figure 6).</p>
<p>The parietal lobe is essential for integrating information from the body’s senses to allow us to build a coherent picture of the world around us.</p>
<p>These lobes allow us to perceive our bodies through somatosensory information (e.g., through touch, pressure, and temperature). It can also help with visuospatial processing, reading, and number representations (mathematics).</p>
<p>The parietal lobes also contain the somatosensory cortex, which receives and processes sensory information, integrating this into a representational map of the body.</p>
<p>This means it can pinpoint the exact area of the body where a sensation is felt, as well as perceive the weight of objects, shape, and texture.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/parietal-lobe-structure.jpg" alt="Parietal Lobe Structure (Simply Psychology)" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 6"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 6.</em> Parietal lobe structure.</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Occipital lobes</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/occipital-lobe.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">occipital lobes</a> are located at the back of the brain behind the temporal and parietal lobes and below the occipital bone of the skull (Figure 7).</p>
<p>The occipital lobes receive sensory information from the eyes&#8217; retinas, which is then encoded into different visual data. Some of the functions of the occipital lobes include being able to assess the size, depth, and distance, determine color information, object and facial recognition, and mapping the visual world.</p>
<p>The occipital lobes also contain the primary visual cortex, which receives sensory information from the retinas, transmitting this information relating to location, spatial data, motion, and the colors of objects in the field of vision.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/occipital-lobe-structure.jpg" alt="Occipital Lobe Structure (Simply Psychology)" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 7"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 7.</em> Occipital lobe structure.</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Cerebral-Cortex"></span>Cerebral Cortex<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>The surface of the cerebrum is called the <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/what-is-the-cerebral-cortex.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">cerebral cortex</a> and has a wrinkled appearance, consisting of bulges, also known as <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/gyri-and-sulci-of-the-brain.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">gyri, and deep furrows, known as sulci</a> (Figure 8).</p>
<p>A gyrus (plural: gyri) is the name given to the bumps and ridges on the cerebral cortex (the outermost layer of the brain). A sulcus (plural: sulci) is another name for a groove in the cerebral cortex.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/gyrus-and-sulcus.jpg" alt="The cortex contains neurons (grey matter), which are interconnected to other brain areas by axons (white matter). The cortex has a folded appearance. A fold is called a gyrus and the valley between is a sulcus." title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 8"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 8.</em> The cortex contains neurons (grey matter) interconnected to other brain areas by axons (white matter). The cortex has a folded appearance. A fold is called a gyrus, and the valley between is a sulcus.</small></p>
<p>The cerebral cortex is primarily constructed of grey matter (neural tissue made up of neurons), with between 14 and 16 billion <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/neuron.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">neurons</a> found here.</p>
<p>The many folds and wrinkles of the cerebral cortex allow a wider surface area for an increased number of neurons to live there, permitting large amounts of information to be processed.</p>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Deep-Structures"></span>Deep Structures<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<div class="panel panel-primary">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Amygdala</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/amygdala.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">amygdala</a> is a structure deep in the brain that is involved in the processing of emotions and fear learning. The amygdala is a part of the limbic system, a neural network that mediates emotion and memory (Figure 9).</p>
<p>This structure also ties emotional meaning to memories, processes rewards, and helps us make decisions. This structure has also been linked with the fight-or-flight response.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/limbic-system.jpg" alt="The amygdala in the limbic system plays a key role in how animals assess and respond to environmental threats and challenges by 
 evaluating the emotional importance of sensory information and prompting an appropriate response." title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 9"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 9.</em> The amygdala in the <a href="limbic-system.html">limbic system</a> plays a key role in how animals assess and respond to environmental threats and challenges by evaluating the emotional importance of sensory information and prompting an appropriate response.</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-primary">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Thalamus and Hypothalamus</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/thalamus.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">thalamus</a> relays information between the cerebral cortex, brain stem, and other cortical structures (Figure 10).</p>
<p>Because of its interactive role in relaying sensory and motor information, the thalamus contributes to many processes, including attention, perception, timing, and movement. The hypothalamus modulates a range of behavioral and physiological functions.</p>
<p>It controls autonomic functions such as hunger, thirst, body temperature, and sexual activity. To do this, the hypothalamus integrates information from different brain parts and responds to various stimuli such as light, odor, and stress.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/Thalamus.jpg" alt="The thalamus is often described as the relay station of the brain as a great deal of information that reaches the cerebral cortex, first stops in the thalamus before being sent to its destination. " title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 10"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 10.</em> The thalamus is often described as the brain&#8217;s relay station as a great deal of information that reaches the cerebral cortex first stops in the thalamus before being sent to its destination. </small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-primary">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Hippocampus</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/hippocampus.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">hippocampus</a> is a curved-shaped structure in the limbic system associated with learning and memory (Figure 11).</p>
<p>This structure is most strongly associated with the formation of memories, is an early storage system for new long-term memories, and plays a role in the transition of these long-term memories to more permanent memories.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/Hippocampus.jpg" alt="Hippocampus location in the brain" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 11"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px; text-align: center;"><small> <em> Figure 11.</em> Hippocampus location in the brain</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-primary">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Basal Ganglia</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>The basal ganglia are a group of structures that regulate the coordination of fine motor movements, balance, and posture alongside the cerebellum.</p>
<p>These structures are connected to other motor areas and link the thalamus with the motor cortex. The basal ganglia are also involved in cognitive and emotional behaviors, as well as playing a role in reward and addiction.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/Basal-Ganglia.jpg" alt="The Basal Ganglia Illustration." title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 12"></p>
<p style="margin-top: -10px;"><small> <em> Figure 12.</em> The Basal Ganglia Illustration</small></p>
</div>
</div>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Ventricles-and-Cerebrospinal-Fluid"></span>Ventricles and Cerebrospinal Fluid<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>Within the brain, there are fluid-filled interconnected cavities called <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/brain-ventricles.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">ventricles</a>, which are extensions of the spinal cord. These are filled with a substance called cerebrospinal fluid, which is a clear and colorless liquid.</p>
<p>The ventricles produce cerebrospinal fluid and transport and remove this fluid. The ventricles do not have a unique function, but they provide cushioning to the brain and are useful for determining the locations of other brain structures.</p>
<p>Cerebrospinal fluid circulates the brain and spinal cord and functions to cushion the brain within the skull. If damage occurs to the skull, the cerebrospinal fluid will act as a shock absorber to help protect the brain from injury.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/cerebrospinal-fluid.jpg" alt="Human" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 13"></p>
<p>As well as providing cushioning, the cerebrospinal fluid circulates nutrients and chemicals filtered from the blood and removes waste products from the brain. Cerebrospinal fluid is constantly absorbed and replenished by the ventricles.</p>
<p>If there were a disruption or blockage, this can cause a build-up of cerebrospinal fluid and can cause enlarged ventricles.</p>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Neurons"></span>Neurons<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<p>Neurons are the <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/neuron.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">nerve cells</a> of the central nervous system that transmit information through electrochemical signals throughout the body. Neurons contain a soma, a cell body from which the axon extends.</p>
<p>Axons are nerve fibers that are the longest part of the neuron, which conduct electrical impulses away from the soma.</p>
<p><img loading="lazy" decoding="async" class="aligncenter wp-image-8253 size-large" src="https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath-1024x528.jpg" alt="Diagram of Neuron Anatomy" width="1024" height="528" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 14" srcset="https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath-1024x528.jpg 1024w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath-300x155.jpg 300w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath-768x396.jpg 768w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath-1536x792.jpg 1536w, https://www.simplypsychology.org/wp-content/uploads/Neuron-with-myelin-sheath.jpg 1920w" sizes="auto, (max-width: 1024px) 100vw, 1024px" /></p>
<p>There are dendrites at the end of the neuron, which are branch-like structures that send and receive information from other neurons.</p>
<p>A myelin sheath, a fatty insulating layer, forms around the axon, allowing nerve impulses to travel down the axon quickly.</p>
<p>There are different types of neurons. Sensory neurons transmit sensory information, motor neurons transmit motor information, and relay neurons allow sensory and motor neurons to communicate.</p>
<p>The communication between neurons is called synapses. Neurons communicate with each other via synaptic clefts, which are gaps between the endings of neurons.</p>
<p><img decoding="async" class="img-responsive img-thumbnail  aligncenter" src="/wp-content/uploads/chemical-synapse.jpg" alt="Transmission of the nerve signal between two neurons with axon and synapse. Close-up of a chemical synapse" title="Parts of the Brain: Anatomy, Structure &amp; Functions 15"></p>
<p>During synaptic transmission, chemicals, such as neurotransmitters, are released from the endings of the previous neuron (also known as the presynaptic neuron).</p>
<p>These chemicals enter the synaptic cleft to then be transported to receptors on the next neuron (also known as the postsynaptic neuron).</p>
<p>Once transported to the next neuron, the chemical messengers continue traveling down neurons to influence many functions, such as behavior and movement.</p>
<h2><span class="ez-toc-section" id="Glial-Cells"></span>Glial Cells<span class="ez-toc-section-end"></span></h2>
<blockquote>
<p><a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/glial-cells.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">Glial cells</a> are non-neuronal cells in the central nervous system which work to provide the neurons with nourishment, support, and protection.</p>
</blockquote>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Astrocytes</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>These are star-shaped cells that function to maintain the environment for neuronal signaling by controlling the levels of neurotransmitters surrounding the synapses.</p>
<p>They also work to clean up what is left behind after synaptic transmission, either recycling any leftover neurotransmitters or cleaning up when a neuron dies.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Oligodendrocytes</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>These types of glial have the appearance of balls with spikes all around them. They function by wrapping around the axons of neurons to form a protective layer called the myelin sheath.</p>
<p>This is a substance that is rich in fat and provides insulation to the neurons to aid neuronal signaling.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Microglial</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>Microglial cells have oval bodies and many branches projecting out of them. The primary function of these cells is to respond to injuries or diseases in the central nervous system.</p>
<p>They respond by clearing away any dead cells or removing any harmful toxins or pathogens that may be present, so they are, therefore, important to the brain&#8217;s health.</p>
</div>
</div>
<div class="panel panel-default">
<div class="panel-heading">
<h3>Ependymal cells</h3>
</div>
<div class="panel-body">
<p>These cells are column-shaped and usually line up together to form a membrane called the ependyma. The ependyma is a thin membrane lining the spinal cord and <a href="https://www.simplypsychology.org/brain-ventricles.html" data-schema-attribute="mentions">ventricles of the brain</a>.</p>
<p>In the ventricles, these cells have small hairlike structures called cilia, which help encourage the flow of cerebrospinal fluid.</p>
</div>
</div>

These allow sensory information to pass from the organs of the face to the brain:</p>
